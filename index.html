Younger Veterans at Higher Risk of Suicidal Tendencies

By . SARAH KREITZER
Nov. 6, 2018

Recent studies are showing an increased susceptibility to mental illnesses and a higher likelihood of death by suicide in veterans ages 18 - 29, specifically men and women who were in post 9-11 or Gulf War combat.

Suicide rates have spiked around 30% in around 25 states since 1999, increasing in almost every state, according to the CDC. (7) And while it’s been a commonly stated statistic that veterans are more likely to commit suicide, recent studies have shown a sharp increase in the probability of veteran death by suicide. In one year, the rate of suicide in veterans under the age of 35 rose drastically.

An extensive study conducted by the Veterans Health Association in 2014 shows that compared to the average U.S. citizens, veterans have a 22% higher risk of dying from suicide.

In contrast to the trend in U.S. male civilians, which shows an increase in suicide related death with age, the rate of suicide in male veterans is highest in the younger population. In addition, trends show higher suicide rates in those aged 30 - 49. The same trend is true for women - while U.S. civilian women suicide rates were highest in women 40 - 59, in women veterans the highest risk was between ages 18 - 29 and 40 - 49.

According to the VHA, continued research is being conducted on why these age groups are at a higher vulnerability. One theory is that veterans from the Iraqi and Gulf War are more vulnerable to PTSD due to the nature of guerilla tactics used (5). In addition, veterans with PTSD, traumatic brain injuries and chronic pain, or what researchers refer to as the “polytraumatic clinical triad”, are at at increased risk of suicidal behavior. PTSD, in combination with depression and substance abuse, has also been shown to drastically increase in suicidal behavior in veterans - many veterans, though not intending to commit suicide, participated in self-harm.

The VA says the are actively working to aid veterans at risk by providing health care services and mental health care, as well as a suicide hotline. However, data shows that though there has been an increase in enrollment in the VA healthcare in the last several years, only around 62% of those enrolled actually utilized their services.

The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline is 1-800-273-8255.
